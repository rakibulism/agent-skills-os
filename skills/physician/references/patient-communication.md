# Patient communication

Accurate information delivered poorly does little good. Communication is a clinical skill: it changes understanding, adherence, anxiety, and outcomes.

## Match the audience

- **Layperson:** plain words, no unexplained jargon ("high blood pressure," not "hypertension," then teach the term once). Lead with what it means for them and what to do.
- **Clinician/student:** precise terminology, mechanism, and the evidence; depth is welcome.
- Check understanding rather than assuming it ("teach-back": ask them to say it back in their own words).

## Structure for explaining a diagnosis or plan

1. **Headline first.** The main message in one sentence, before the detail. ("The tests point to a chest infection, and antibiotics should help.")
2. **What it is** — a brief, concrete explanation in their terms.
3. **What it means for them** — implications for daily life, prognosis in honest but humane terms.
4. **What happens next** — the plan and their role in it.
5. **Safety-net** — exactly what should prompt them to seek care again, and how urgently.
6. **Space for questions** — invite them; people often hold the real worry until asked.

## Breaking bad or serious news (SPIKES)

- **S**etting — privacy, time, sit down, no interruptions.
- **P**erception — find out what they already understand.
- **I**nvitation — ask how much they want to know.
- **K**nowledge — warn ("I'm afraid I have some difficult news"), then say it simply; pause.
- **E**motions — respond to feelings with empathy before facts; allow silence.
- **S**trategy & summary — agree the next steps; check understanding.

## Principles

- **Lead with empathy when there's distress.** Acknowledge the feeling before piling on information; an anxious person can't absorb a lecture.
- **Be honest about uncertainty** without abandoning the person in it. "We don't know yet, and here's how we'll find out" beats false reassurance.
- **Chunk and check.** Small pieces, confirm understanding, then continue. Don't deliver everything at once.
- **Respect autonomy.** Lay out options and trade-offs; support the person's informed choice rather than dictating. Their values are part of the right answer.
- **Numbers humanely.** Use absolute terms and natural frequencies ("about 1 in 100 people"), offer both the chance of benefit and of harm, and avoid framing that pushes a decision.

## When the person is describing their own symptoms
Help them understand what the symptoms might mean and, crucially, **what merits attention and when** — framed as "reasons to see a clinician" and "questions to ask," not a diagnosis. If anything suggests an emergency, say so first and direct to urgent care. Be warm; they came worried.
