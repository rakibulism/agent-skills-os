# Differential diagnosis

A differential is a prioritized list of conditions that could explain the presentation. The discipline is to be both *broad* (so you don't miss things) and *prioritized* (so you act on what's likely and what's dangerous).

## Build it broad with a sieve

To avoid tunnel vision, generate causes systematically rather than free-associating. Two common scaffolds:

**VINDICATE**
- **V**ascular, **I**nfective/Inflammatory, **N**eoplastic, **D**egenerative, **I**atrogenic/Idiopathic, **C**ongenital, **A**utoimmune, **T**raumatic, **E**ndocrine/metabolic.

**Anatomical** — work through the structures in the region from skin inward, or organ by organ. (For abdominal pain: walk the anatomy — wall, peritoneum, each organ in each quadrant.)

Use whichever surfaces the broadest sensible list for the problem.

## Then prioritize on two axes

For each candidate, judge:
1. **Likelihood** — given this patient (age, sex, risk factors, exposures) and base rates. "Common things are common."
2. **Danger** — what happens if it's this and you miss it.

Order the differential by likelihood, but **always flag the "can't-miss" diagnoses** — the high-danger conditions you must actively exclude even when unlikely (e.g., for chest pain: ACS, PE, aortic dissection, tension pneumothorax, oesophageal rupture). You don't have to think they're probable; you have to make sure you've considered and addressed them.

## Discriminate between hypotheses

- **Pertinent positives and negatives** — for each leading diagnosis, what features would you expect, and are they present or absent? Absences are evidence.
- **Discriminating features** — find the symptom/sign/test that best separates the top contenders, and seek it. Efficient reasoning targets the question that splits the differential.
- **Pattern fit** — how completely does the case match each illness script? Note the parts that don't fit; unexplained features often hold the answer.

## Investigations that earn their place

Order a test only if its result would change what you do:
- To **confirm** a likely diagnosis,
- To **exclude** a dangerous one,
- To **assess severity** or guide treatment.

Tie each test to the hypotheses it moves. Avoid the reflexive panel that generates incidental findings and anxiety without changing management. Remember every test has false positives and negatives — interpret in light of pre-test probability (see evidence-based-medicine).

## Re-evaluate
A differential is provisional. If the response to treatment or new data doesn't fit the working diagnosis, revisit the list — don't force the patient into the wrong script. The willingness to reopen the differential is what catches the missed diagnosis.

> Reminder: in this skill, differentials are for reasoning and education. Diagnosing and treating a real patient requires an examining clinician.
