# Evidence-based medicine

EBM is the integration of the best available evidence, clinical expertise, and the patient's values. Each leg matters: evidence without judgment is rote; judgment without evidence is anecdote; both without the patient's values miss the point.

## Test characteristics (the probabilistic core)

- **Sensitivity** — of those *with* the disease, the fraction the test catches. A highly sensitive test, when **negative**, helps rule a disease *out* (SnNout).
- **Specificity** — of those *without* the disease, the fraction correctly cleared. A highly specific test, when **positive**, helps rule a disease *in* (SpPin).
- **Predictive values depend on prevalence.** The same test gives very different positive predictive value in a high-risk clinic vs. a low-risk screen. A positive result in a low-prevalence setting is often a false positive.
- **Likelihood ratios** combine both and are prevalence-independent: LR+ = sens/(1−spec), LR− = (1−sens)/spec. Multiply pre-test odds by the LR to get post-test odds. LR+ >10 or LR− <0.1 meaningfully shifts probability; near 1 changes little.

## Bayesian updating in words

Start with the pre-test probability (base rate + this patient's specifics). A test result *updates* it — it rarely sets it to 0 or 100%. Always ask: what was the probability before the test, and how much does this result actually move it? A "positive" screening test on a low-risk person may still leave the disease unlikely.

## The evidence hierarchy (appraise, then apply)

Roughly strongest to weakest for therapy questions:
1. Systematic reviews / meta-analyses of RCTs
2. Individual well-conducted RCTs
3. Cohort studies
4. Case-control studies
5. Case series / reports
6. Expert opinion, mechanistic reasoning

Hierarchy is a guide, not a hammer: a sloppy RCT can be worse than a strong cohort, and some questions (harms, rare events, prognosis) are better answered by observational designs. The right design depends on the question.

## Appraising a study (quickly)

- **Validity:** randomized? allocation concealed? blinded? intention-to-treat? complete follow-up? Are the groups comparable?
- **Results:** what's the effect *size*, not just significance? **Absolute** risk reduction (and **NNT** = 1/ARR) is more honest than relative risk reduction, which can dramatize small effects.
- **Applicability:** were the trial patients like the one in front of you? Were the outcomes ones that matter to patients (mortality, function) or surrogate markers?
- **Conflicts and bias:** funding, selective outcome reporting, publication bias.

## Numbers that mislead
- **Relative vs. absolute risk** — "halves your risk" sounds huge; if it's 2% → 1%, the absolute benefit is 1%.
- **NNT / NNH** — how many treated for one to benefit / be harmed; grounds the trade-off.
- **Surrogate endpoints** — a drug that improves a lab number may not improve how patients actually do.
- **Lead-time and length-time bias** — make screened survival look better without anyone living longer.

## Putting it together
Guidelines synthesize evidence into recommendations — use current, local ones, and know they lag the literature and require individualization. The art is combining the numbers with this patient's situation and preferences, and being honest about the uncertainty that remains.

> Flag when specifics (doses, thresholds, guideline recommendations) should be verified against current authoritative sources; these change, and this reference is conceptual, not a substitute for up-to-date guidelines.
