# Clinical reasoning

Clinical reasoning is the structured movement from an undifferentiated complaint to a working diagnosis and plan. Good clinicians make it look like pattern recognition; underneath is a disciplined process you can learn.

## The problem representation

Compress the case into one line: **demographics + key qualifying features + temporal pattern**. "A 65-year-old smoker with three weeks of progressive exertional breathlessness and weight loss." This summary primes the right illness scripts and is the single most useful reasoning habit.

## Structured history

History contributes the majority of diagnoses. A focused, complete history:

- **HPC (history of presenting complaint)** — the story of the symptom over time. For pain use **OPQRST**: Onset (sudden/gradual), Provocation/Palliation (what makes it better/worse), Quality (sharp/dull/crushing), Region/Radiation, Severity (0–10), Timing (constant/intermittent, duration, progression).
- **Associated symptoms & pertinent negatives** — both what's present and the important absences (a pertinent negative can rule a diagnosis up or down).
- **PMH** (past medical/surgical history), **Drugs** (including OTC, supplements) and **Allergies**, **Family history**, **Social history** (smoking, alcohol, occupation, travel, living situation), and a **systems review** for anything missed.
- **Ideas, Concerns, Expectations (ICE)** — what the patient thinks is going on, what worries them, what they hope for. Improves both accuracy and trust.

## Illness scripts

An illness script is the stored profile of a disease: predisposing conditions → pathophysiology → typical presentation (symptoms, signs, time course) → usual investigations/response. Expertise is a large, well-organized library of scripts. When a patient's features match a script, you have a hypothesis; mismatches ("the fever doesn't fit") are clues that sharpen or redirect it.

## Dual-process thinking and bias

- **System 1** (fast, intuitive pattern matching) is efficient and usually right for familiar presentations.
- **System 2** (slow, analytical) is for atypical, complex, or high-stakes cases.
- Deliberately switch to System 2 — slow down and reason through the differential — when the case is unusual, when you feel certain very quickly, or when the stakes are high. That's how you guard against:
  - **Anchoring** — fixating on an initial impression.
  - **Premature closure** — stopping once one diagnosis fits, before checking it's the best fit.
  - **Confirmation bias** — seeking data that confirm, ignoring data that refute.
  - **Availability bias** — over-weighting a recently seen or memorable diagnosis.
  - **Search satisficing** — finding one abnormality and stopping (the second fracture, the co-ingestion).

A useful counter-habit: explicitly ask "what else could this be, and what doesn't fit my leading diagnosis?"

## The diagnostic loop

Gather data → generate hypotheses → test hypotheses with targeted history/exam/investigations → refine → arrive at a working diagnosis → treat → **re-evaluate against response**. Diagnosis is iterative; the patient's response to treatment is itself data. Always build in a safety-net: define what would prompt reassessment and ensure the patient knows it.
