# Methodology

The method must serve the question. Choosing a design because it's familiar, then reshaping the question to fit it, is the most common methodological error. Decide what kind of knowledge the question demands, then pick accordingly.

## The big choice: quantitative, qualitative, mixed

- **Quantitative** — for questions of how much, how many, how often, and whether X causes/predicts Y. Numeric data, statistical analysis, generalizable, tests hypotheses. Strength: precision and generalization. Weakness: thin on meaning and mechanism.
- **Qualitative** — for questions of how and why, of meaning, process, and lived experience. Text/observation data, interpretive analysis, deep but local. Strength: depth, context, theory-building. Weakness: limited generalization, interpreter-dependent.
- **Mixed methods** — combine to get both breadth and depth (e.g., survey to map the landscape, interviews to explain the surprising pattern). Be explicit about how the strands integrate, not just co-exist.

## Common designs

**Quantitative**
- Experimental (RCT): random assignment → strongest causal claims.
- Quasi-experimental: comparison without randomization (difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, instrumental variables) when you can't randomize.
- Correlational/survey: measures associations in observed data; no causal claim without strong assumptions.
- Longitudinal vs. cross-sectional: over time vs. a snapshot.

**Qualitative**
- Interviews (structured → unstructured); focus groups.
- Ethnography / participant observation.
- Case study (single or comparative).
- Grounded theory (build theory from data); thematic analysis; discourse/content analysis.

## Sampling

- **Probability sampling** (random, stratified, cluster) supports generalization — necessary for quantitative inference.
- **Purposive/theoretical sampling** (information-rich cases) suits qualitative depth.
- Justify sample size: power analysis for quantitative; saturation (new data stops yielding new themes) for qualitative.
- Name the population you can actually generalize to — usually narrower than you'd like.

## Validity and reliability

- **Internal validity** — can you attribute the outcome to the cause you claim? Threatened by confounds, selection, attrition (see the scientist skill's experiment-design reference).
- **External validity** — does it generalize beyond this sample/setting?
- **Construct validity** — does your measure actually capture the concept? (A survey item about "stress" may capture something else.)
- **Reliability** — would the measurement repeat? (inter-rater agreement, test-retest, internal consistency.)
- For qualitative work, the parallel virtues are **credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability** — pursued via triangulation, member checking, thick description, and an audit trail.

## Reproducibility and ethics

- **Pre-register** confirmatory hypotheses and analyses where the field supports it; distinguish them from exploratory analyses.
- **Document** data, code, and decisions so another researcher could reproduce the work.
- **Ethics:** informed consent, minimization of harm, privacy/anonymity, conflict-of-interest disclosure, and IRB/ethics-board approval where humans or animals are involved.

## State limitations honestly

Every design trades something. Name what yours can't establish (e.g., "this correlational design cannot establish causation," "findings from one university may not transfer"). Reviewers trust authors who pre-empt the obvious objections.
