# Scholarly writing

Academic writing prizes precision, attribution, and appropriate hedging over style for its own sake. The reader should always know what's established, what's claimed, and what you're arguing.

## Structure: IMRaD (empirical work)

- **Introduction** — the problem, why it matters, what's known, the gap, your question/contribution. Funnel from broad to specific; end on the precise question or hypotheses.
- **Methods** — what you did, in enough detail to reproduce. Past tense, no justof-results.
- **Results** — what you found, reported neutrally (tables/figures + the key numbers). No interpretation here.
- **and**
- **Discussion** — what it means, how it fits prior work, limitations, implications, future work. This is where interpretation lives — clearly separated from results.

Non-empirical fields vary (humanities use thesis-driven essay structures), but the principle holds: signpost the argument and keep evidence distinct from interpretation.

## The abstract

A miniature of the whole: context, question, method, key result, significance — typically 150–250 words. Write it last. It's the most-read part; make it carry the paper.

## Citation discipline

- **Attribute every borrowed idea, fact, statistic, method, and phrasing.** The reader must be able to find the source.
- **Never fabricate a citation.** Do not invent authors, years, titles, journals, DOIs, quotes, or statistics, and do not attribute a position to a source you haven't verified. If a needed citation is uncertain, flag it explicitly as "[citation needed / verify]" rather than inventing one. This is non-negotiable — a single fake reference destroys credibility.
- **Quote vs. paraphrase.** Quote when the exact wording matters; paraphrase (genuinely reworded, not patchwritten) otherwise — and cite either way.
- **Use the field's style consistently** (APA, MLA, Chicago, IEEE, Vancouver). Match in-text citations to a complete reference list.

## Voice and hedging

- **Calibrate verbs to evidence:** *shows/demonstrates* (strong, for your robust results) vs. *suggests/indicates/is consistent with* (for tentative or correlational findings) vs. *may/might/could* (speculation). Overclaiming is the fastest path to rejection.
- **Keep your voice separate from sources'.** The reader should always know whether a sentence reports the literature or advances your argument.
- **Be precise, not ornate.** Prefer the exact term to the impressive one. Define jargon on first use.
- **Concede and respond.** Strong scholarly writing anticipates the best counterargument and addresses it, rather than ignoring it.

## Limitations and integrity

End empirical work with honest limitations — sample, design, measurement, generalizability. Disclose conflicts of interest and funding. Report what didn't work, not only what did. Integrity is the currency; spend it carefully.

## Revision

First drafts over-explain and under-argue. Cut throat-clearing ("It is important to note that…"), tighten claims to match evidence, verify every citation resolves to a real source, and make sure the abstract, introduction, and conclusion tell the same story.
