# Research questions

The research question is the spine of the project. Get it right and the methods, scope, and contribution follow. Get it wrong and no amount of work fixes it.

## From topic to question

A topic is a noun phrase ("remote work and productivity"). A research question is interrogative, bounded, and answerable. Narrow along these axes until it's tractable:

- **Population:** who/what exactly? ("knowledge workers," not "people")
- **Variables/concepts:** which constructs, measured how?
- **Scope:** where, when, under what conditions?
- **Relationship:** describe, compare, correlate, or cause?

Example progression:
- Topic: remote work and productivity.
- Narrower: does remote work affect productivity?
- Researchable: Among software engineers at mid-size firms, is fully-remote work associated with differences in self-reported and commit-based productivity over 12 months, compared with hybrid work?

## Criteria for a good question (FINER)

- **Feasible** — answerable with available data, time, methods, and ethics.
- **Interesting** — to you and to the field.
- **Novel** — adds something; not already settled.
- **Ethical** — can be studied without undue harm.
- **Relevant** — matters to scholarship, practice, or policy.

## Operationalizing with PICO/PECO

For empirical questions, force the structure:
- **P**opulation — who is studied.
- **I**ntervention / **E**xposure — what is done or what differs.
- **C**omparison — against what baseline/group.
- **O**utcome — what is measured, and how.

If you can't fill all four, the question isn't ready.

## Question types and what they demand

| Type | Asks | Typical method |
|---|---|---|
| Descriptive | what/how much/how often | survey, observation, descriptive stats |
| Relational | is X associated with Y | correlational, regression |
| Causal | does X cause Y | experiment/RCT, quasi-experiment, causal inference |
| Mechanistic | how/why does X lead to Y | process tracing, mediation, qualitative |
| Interpretive | what does X mean to whom | qualitative, ethnography, discourse analysis |

## Hypotheses (for confirmatory work)

State directional, falsifiable hypotheses with a null, and pre-register them when possible to separate confirmatory tests from exploratory findings. Exploratory analysis is legitimate and valuable — just label it as such; don't dress it up as a prediction you made in advance.

## A good question is done when

It fits in one sentence, names the population and the relationship, could be answered (in principle) by a study you can describe, and a peer would agree it's worth answering.
