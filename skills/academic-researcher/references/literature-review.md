# Literature review

A literature review is an argument about the state of knowledge, not a stack of summaries. Its job is to show what's known, where the field disagrees, and where the gap is that your work fills.

## Searching well

- **Start broad, then systematic.** Identify key terms and their synonyms; search several databases (don't rely on one engine). Track your search terms and counts so the process is reproducible (PRISMA-style).
- **Follow citation trails both ways.** Backward (who did this paper cite?) and forward (who has cited it since?). The seminal works and the latest works both matter.
- **Find the reviews first.** A recent systematic review or survey paper is a map of the territory and a source of references — but read the primary sources before you cite them.
- **Note for each source:** the question, method, sample, key finding, limitations, and how it relates to your question. Record the full citation immediately — reconstructing references later wastes hours and breeds errors.

## Screening

Decide inclusion/exclusion criteria up front (date range, study type, population, quality threshold) and apply them consistently. Be transparent about what you excluded and why. Engage strong work that contradicts your thesis — omitting inconvenient studies is cherry-picking.

## Organizing: by theme, not by paper

The novice review reads "Smith (2019) found… Jones (2021) found…" — a list. A real review is organized around **themes, debates, and developments**:

- **Thematic** — group by sub-topic or concept.
- **Debate-centered** — organize around the open disagreements ("camp A argues X; camp B argues Y; the evidence on each…").
- **Chronological/developmental** — when the story is how thinking evolved.
- **Methodological** — when the interesting variation is in how the question's been studied.

Within each, **synthesize**: what do these works collectively establish, where do they conflict, what explains the conflict, and what remains open?

## Finding and naming the gap

Articulate precisely what's missing:
- **Evidence gap** — claims that lack adequate empirical support.
- **Knowledge gap** — a question simply not yet asked.
- **Methodological gap** — prior work limited by its methods (small samples, weak designs, one population).
- **Population/context gap** — studied in one group/place, untested elsewhere.
- **Theoretical gap** — phenomena no existing theory explains well.

Your contribution is defined by the gap. State it as a sentence: "Although X is well established, no study has Y; this work addresses that by Z."

## Critiquing a paper (and your own)

```
- Question: clear, significant, answerable?
- Positioning: engages the relevant prior work fairly?
- Method: appropriate to the question? sample adequate? design valid?
- Validity threats: internal, external, construct, statistical — addressed?
- Findings: supported by the data? overclaimed?
- Limitations: acknowledged honestly?
- Ethics: consent, harm, conflicts, data handling?
- Contribution: does it actually advance the field?
```

## Synthesis is the deliverable

The reader should finish your review knowing the shape of the field, the live controversies, and exactly why your question is the one worth answering next — not just a catalogue of who said what.
