# Valuation, dilution & term sheets

A great company can be a mediocre investment at the wrong price or terms, and a good price can't save a broken business. Returns come from outcome × ownership ÷ dilution — all three matter.

> Educational overview only. Term sheets have legal and financial consequences; real deals need qualified legal and financial counsel.

## The core arithmetic

- **Pre-money** = company's value before the new investment. **Post-money** = pre-money + amount raised.
- **Ownership bought** = investment ÷ post-money. (Invest $2M at $8M pre → $10M post → 20%.)
- **Dilution:** every later round issues new shares, shrinking earlier holders' percentages (unless they invest pro-rata to maintain ownership). Model the full path to exit, not just this round.
- **Return contribution** ≈ entry ownership × exit value × (1 − future dilution). Small ownership in a huge outcome can still underwhelm — which is why entry ownership and reserves matter.

## Valuation at early stage

Early valuations are negotiated more than calculated — driven by stage norms, comparable rounds, team/market quality, traction, and competition for the deal — not DCF. Sanity-check against: comparable recent rounds at the same stage, the ownership the round implies for investors, and whether the post-money sets up a *clean* next round (a too-high price now can cause a painful down-round later). Price discipline matters because you're underwriting upside: paying 3× the fair entry price cuts your multiple by ~3×.

## Instruments

- **Priced equity round** — sets a valuation, issues preferred shares now.
- **SAFE / convertible note** — invest now, convert to equity at the next priced round, usually with a **valuation cap** (max conversion price) and/or **discount**. Stacked SAFEs can cause surprising dilution — always model conversion on a fully-diluted basis.

## Key economic terms

- **Liquidation preference** — investors get their money back (1× is standard; >1× or *participating* preferences are aggressive) before common shareholders in an exit. Multiple/participating prefs can mean founders/common get little in a modest exit even with meaningful ownership.
- **Pro-rata rights** — the right to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership; valuable for protecting stake in winners.
- **Option pool** — shares reserved for employees, usually carved out of the *pre-money* (so founders, not new investors, bear the dilution). The "pool shuffle" effectively lowers the real pre-money — watch for it.
- **Anti-dilution** — protects investors if a later round prices lower (broad-based weighted average is normal; full-ratchet is harsh).

## Key control terms

- **Board composition** — who controls the board now and after future rounds.
- **Protective provisions** — investor veto rights over major decisions (sale, new financing, budget).
- **Founder vesting** — founders earn shares over time (typically 4 years, 1-year cliff), aligning incentives and protecting against early departures.
- **Drag-along / tag-along, information rights, pro-rata** — round out the standard package.

## Reading a term sheet
Separate **economics** (who gets what money) from **control** (who decides what). A founder-friendly headline valuation can hide punishing preferences or control terms; a modest valuation with clean terms can be the better deal. Model the cap table through to a few exit scenarios (small, medium, home-run) and see who actually makes what in each.

## The investor's discipline
Decide your price and terms walk-away in advance. Be willing to pass on a good company at a bad price — discipline on entry is one of the few things an investor fully controls, and it compounds across a portfolio.
