# Deal evaluation

Evaluating an early-stage deal is the disciplined version of a simple question: could this be one of the rare companies that returns the fund, and is the probability high enough to bet on at this price? Work the lattice below, weighting team and market most when there's little else to see.

## Team (the dominant early-stage factor)

With little product or traction to judge, you're mostly betting on people.
- **Founder-market fit** — why *these* founders for *this* problem? Earned insight, domain depth, or an unfair advantage beats generic talent.
- **Exceptional execution** — velocity (how much they've done with how little), clarity of thought, and how fast they learn. Talk to them twice and watch the delta.
- **Recruiting & magnetism** — can they attract great people and customers? A founder who can't recruit caps the company.
- **Resilience & integrity** — startups are years of adversity; and you're entering a long, high-trust relationship. Back-channel references beyond the ones offered.
- **Coachability vs. conviction** — the balance of listening and holding a strong, differentiated view.

## Market

A great team in a tiny or no market rarely produces a venture outcome. See `market-sizing.md`. Key questions: is it large or becoming large, is it growing, and **why now** (what technological, regulatory, or behavioral shift makes this the moment)?

## Product & wedge

- The **insight**: what does this team believe that others don't, that turns out to be right?
- The **wedge**: the sharp initial use case that gets a foothold, and the **expansion path** from wedge to a much bigger platform.
- **Evidence of love**: retention, organic pull, NPS, usage intensity — do customers *need* it or merely tolerate it?

## Traction (read it skeptically)

- **Growth rate and retention** over raw totals; **cohorts** over aggregates.
- **Quality of revenue**: recurring vs. one-off, organic vs. paid, concentrated vs. diversified, gross margin.
- **What's omitted**: a deck that shows cumulative signups but not retention is hiding the retention. Ask for what's missing; the gaps are the story.

## Moat / defensibility

Why won't this be competed away once it's proven? Network effects, scale economies, switching costs, brand, proprietary data/tech, regulatory advantage. At early stage this is usually a *thesis* about a moat the company could build, not a moat it has. Judge plausibility.

## The bull and bear, honestly

Write both:
- **Bull:** if everything goes right, how big — and is that fund-returning?
- **Bear:** the most likely ways it dies or stays small (no PMF, market too small, competition, founder issues, economics don't work, can't raise the next round).

Then state the **specific outcome you're underwriting** and what would have to be true. If the bull case isn't large enough to matter to the fund, it's usually a pass regardless of how solid it looks.

## Risks and de-risking

List the top risks and, for each, the milestone or evidence that would retire it. Good rounds are sized to reach the milestones that retire the *next* biggest risk. If a risk can't be retired by the next round, that's a structural concern.

## The decision

Conclude: invest / pass / track-and-revisit. Give the reasons, the price/terms sensitivity ("yes at this valuation, no at 2×"), and the one or two things that, if learned, would flip your answer. Reasoning in probabilities and disconfirming evidence beats conviction theater.
