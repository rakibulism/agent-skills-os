# Market sizing

Market size answers the question that power-law math demands: if this works, can it get big enough to matter? Size it honestly — most decks inflate it, and most investors discount the deck's number heavily.

## TAM / SAM / SOM

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** — total demand if you had 100% of everyone who could ever use this. The ceiling.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)** — the slice your product and business model can actually serve (segment, geography, channel).
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)** — what you can realistically capture in a few years given competition and execution. The number that matters for near-term reality.

## Bottom-up beats top-down (always)

- **Top-down (red flag):** "The market is $50B; if we get just 2%, that's $1B." This assumes a share with no mechanism. Investors distrust it because it's untethered from how you actually acquire customers.
- **Bottom-up (credible):** number of potential customers × what each pays × frequency. Build from real units: "There are ~200k dental practices in the US; our software is $400/mo; that's a ~$960M annual TAM," then narrow to who you can actually reach and win. Ground each input in evidence.

Bottom-up forces you to confront the unit economics and the acquisition reality, which is exactly what you want to interrogate.

## Beyond the static number

- **Growth rate.** A smaller market growing 40%/year can beat a larger stagnant one. Investors fund where the puck is going.
- **Market creation.** Some of the biggest outcomes addressed markets that didn't visibly exist yet (rideshare, cloud). Here, sizing is a thesis about latent/created demand — judge the behavior-change argument, not a current spreadsheet.
- **"Why now?"** The timing thesis: what shift (technology, cost curve, regulation, behavior, platform) makes this market open *now* when it wasn't before? A strong "why now" is one of the most predictive signals; its absence is a warning.
- **Expansion vectors.** Today's wedge market may be small, but does it open adjacent markets (more segments, products, geographies)? Great companies start in a beachhead and expand. Size both the beachhead and the expansion.

## Common traps

- **Inflated TAM** that counts people who'll never buy. Discount to who genuinely has the problem and budget.
- **Confusing TAM with revenue opportunity for *you*** — competition, share, and pricing power all cut it down.
- **Ignoring willingness to pay** — a huge population that won't pay is not a market.
- **Static thinking** — markets move; size the trajectory, not just the snapshot.

## What good looks like
A credible market case: a bottom-up SOM grounded in real customers and price, a believable expansion path to a large SAM/TAM, a market growing or being created, and a sharp "why now." If the honest SOM can't get large even when things go well, the power-law math probably doesn't work.
