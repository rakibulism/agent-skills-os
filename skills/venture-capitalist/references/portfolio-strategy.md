# Portfolio strategy & returns

A venture fund is a portfolio, and the portfolio — not any single deal — is the unit of success. The power law dictates a counterintuitive strategy: optimize for the magnitude of your winners, not the survival of your average.

## The power law, quantified

Returns are extremely skewed. In a typical fund, a small fraction of investments produce the vast majority of returns; many return ~0. This means:
- **Most deals will fail or return little**, and that's expected, not a process failure.
- **One or two outliers must "return the fund"** (return ≥ the entire fund size on their own) for the fund to succeed.
- Therefore every new investment is judged by: *could this be a fund-returner?* A company that can realistically only 3× is often a pass for a venture fund (though right for other capital), because it can't move a power-law portfolio.

## Construction levers

- **Entry ownership.** Because winners pay for everything, you need enough ownership in them to matter. Funds target a minimum entry ownership (e.g., 10–20% depending on strategy) so a home run is meaningful after dilution.
- **Reserves / follow-on.** Hold capital to invest again in your winners as they raise later rounds — "double down on the ones working." Often a large share of a fund is reserved for follow-on. Pro-rata rights protect ownership in winners against dilution.
- **Shots on goal vs. concentration.** More investments = more chances to catch an outlier, but spreads ownership and attention thin. Fewer, higher-conviction bets concentrate ownership but raise variance. Each fund picks a point on this spectrum (e.g., a focused fund of ~20–30 companies vs. a broad seed portfolio of 100+).
- **Stage and check size** consistent with the fund's size and the ownership math.

## Reserves and the follow-on decision

Following-on is not automatic loyalty — it's a fresh investment decision at the new price. Re-underwrite: is this still a potential fund-returner at the new valuation? Concentrate reserves into the breakouts; resist averaging down into the strugglers ("don't throw good money after bad"). The hardest discipline is cutting losses and pouring into winners, against the emotional pull of the opposite.

## Loss tolerance and conviction

Because the downside of any single deal is capped at 1× (you lose your check) while the upside is effectively uncapped, the asymmetry rewards seeking massive upside even at high failure rates. This is why VCs accept many zeros: a single 100× outweighs dozens of total losses. The mistake is *not* taking enough upside risk — backing safe, modest companies that can't return the fund.

## Anti-portfolio and selection effects

- Track the deals you passed on that became winners (the "anti-portfolio") — it's the most honest feedback on your judgment.
- Beware adverse selection: the deals easiest to get into may be the ones better investors passed on. Access to the best deals (proprietary sourcing, founder reputation) is itself a core skill.

## Fund mechanics (briefly)

- VCs raise from LPs and typically earn **management fees** (~2%/yr) and **carried interest** (~20% of profits, often above a hurdle). Carry aligns the GP with outsized returns.
- Funds have a life (~10 years): an investment period, then a harvest period. Liquidity comes from acquisitions and IPOs, often many years out — venture is illiquid and patient.
- A fund "returns" via DPI (cash actually distributed) and is marked along the way via TVPI/IRR, but paper markups aren't returns until realized.

## The throughline
Every individual decision in this skill — team, market, terms, ownership — ultimately serves one portfolio question: am I assembling enough credible shots at a fund-returning outcome, with enough ownership in the ones that hit? Optimize the portfolio's upside, manage its losses with discipline, and let the power law do the rest.
