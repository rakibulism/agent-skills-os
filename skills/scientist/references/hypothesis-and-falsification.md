# Hypotheses and falsification

A hypothesis earns the name only if reality could contradict it. This file is about writing hypotheses that are sharp enough to be wrong.

## Anatomy of a good hypothesis

- **Specific.** Names the variables and the direction of the expected effect. Not "diet affects health" but "increasing daily fiber by 10g reduces LDL cholesterol within 8 weeks in adults."
- **Falsifiable.** There is an observation that would refute it. "An invisible dragon lives in my garage that you can't detect" is unfalsifiable — and therefore empty.
- **Predictive and discriminating.** It forecasts something you'd see if it's true *and not* if it's false. A hypothesis consistent with every outcome explains none.
- **Mechanistic where possible.** A proposed *why* (the mechanism) is more testable and more useful than a bare correlation, because it generates additional predictions.

## Always write the null

The null hypothesis (H0) is the boring default: no effect, no difference, chance alone. You don't try to prove H1 directly; you ask whether the data are surprising *under H0*. If they're very surprising under H0, that's evidence for H1. Forgetting H0 is how people mistake noise for signal.

## Competing hypotheses, not a pet hypothesis

Good investigation discriminates among several explanations rather than accumulating support for one. For an observed effect, list the candidates — real effect, confound, measurement artifact, selection, chance — and design so that the result points to one and away from the others. This is the method of **strong inference**: at each step, design the test whose outcomes exclude alternatives.

## Falsification in practice

- **Predict, then look.** Commit to the prediction before seeing the data. Pre-registration (writing down hypothesis, analysis, and stopping rule in advance) prevents the mind's after-the-fact storytelling (HARKing).
- **Seek the disconfirming test.** The most informative experiment is the one most likely to break your hypothesis if it's false. Confirmations are cheap; a survived falsification attempt is worth far more.
- **Severe tests.** A test is "severe" if it would very probably have caught the error if the hypothesis were false. Passing a severe test is strong support; passing an easy one is nearly worthless.

## Auxiliary assumptions

Every test relies on background assumptions (instruments work, samples are representative, the model is roughly right). When a prediction fails, the culprit might be the hypothesis *or* an auxiliary assumption. Before discarding a theory, check the scaffolding — but don't endlessly add rescuing assumptions to save a dying hypothesis (that's how astrology survives).

## Theory quality, beyond truth

Among hypotheses that fit the data, prefer those that are: simpler (fewer free parameters), broader (explain more), more precise (forbid more), and fruitful (suggest new tests). A theory that explains the past but predicts nothing new is on life support.
