# Problem framing

The single highest-leverage move in product design is framing the right problem. A well-framed problem makes the solution almost obvious; a badly framed one wastes a quarter.

## Symptoms of an unframed problem

- The request is phrased as a solution ("add a settings page," "we need AI").
- Nobody can say who specifically has the problem or how often.
- The success criterion is "ship it," not a change in user behavior.
- Three people in the room mean three different things by the same feature name.

## A good problem statement

Fill every slot. If a slot is a guess, label it as an assumption to test.

```
WHO: <specific user / segment, not "users">
JOB: When <situation/trigger>, they want to <motivation>, so they can <desired outcome>.
TODAY: They currently <workaround / current behavior>.
COST: This is bad because <time / money / errors / drop-off / emotion> — ideally quantified.
EVIDENCE: We believe this because <data / research / support tickets / sales calls>.
NOT THE PROBLEM: <adjacent things this is explicitly not about>.
```

## Techniques

- **5 Whys** — ask why until you hit a root cause or a value, not a surface complaint. Stop when the next "why" leaves the user's world.
- **The Mom Test** — when citing user evidence, prefer what people *did* over what they *said they'd do*. "Would you use this?" is worthless; "what did you do last time this happened?" is gold.
- **Inversion** — describe the problem from the user's point of view in their words. If it only makes sense in company jargon, you don't understand it yet.
- **Reframe at different altitudes** — "users can't find the export button" (UI) vs. "users can't get their data out to share with their accountant" (job). Solve at the altitude where the most solutions open up.

## Distinguish problem types

- **Painkiller vs. vitamin** — does solving this remove acute pain (urgent, willing to switch/pay) or add marginal nicety (easy to ignore)?
- **Frequency × intensity** — a small pain felt daily can outrank a big pain felt yearly. Map both.
- **Whose problem** — user pain, business pain, and team pain are different. Be explicit about which you're solving; the best initiatives solve user pain *in a way that* relieves business pain.

## The framing is done when

You can state the problem in two sentences a stranger understands, name who has it and how you know, and articulate what specifically gets better — without naming a solution.
