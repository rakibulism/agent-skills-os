# Leadership & people

Most of a founder's hardest moments are human, not technical. The team, the co-founder relationship, and the founder's own conduct as a leader determine far more than any single product decision. And nearly every leadership failure traces back to a conversation that was avoided.

## Hiring

- **Hire for the stage you're in.** Early hires must be generalists, comfortable with ambiguity, and self-directed. The specialist who needs structure thrives later, not now.
- **Bar over speed — but don't agonize.** "Hire slow" means keep the bar high and check references hard, not dither for months. A wrong hire in a small team is enormously costly (culture, morale, your time).
- **Hire for slope, not just intercept.** Trajectory and learning rate often beat current credentials, especially early.
- **Test for values and how they work, not just skills.** Skills can be taught; misalignment on honesty, ownership, and how they treat people cannot.
- **Sell the mission honestly.** Great people join for the problem and the team; don't oversell — mismatched expectations cause early churn.

## Firing & underperformance ("fire fast" — humanely)

- The hardest, most-delayed decision. Keeping the wrong person harms the company, the team (who see it), and often the person (in the wrong seat). Acting decisively is the kindness, done with dignity.
- Before parting: was the expectation clear, the feedback honest and timely, the support real? Sometimes it's a coaching or role-fit problem, not a person problem.
- When it's time: be direct, kind, and prompt. Don't drag it out, don't blindside (there should have been prior honest feedback), protect their dignity, and be generous where you can.
- **"Hire slow, fire fast" is hard precisely because it's emotional.** The job is to feel the weight *and* still act.

## Co-founder relationships

- The co-founder relationship is among the top reasons startups implode. Treat it like a marriage that needs maintenance.
- **Talk about the hard things early:** roles and decision rights, equity (and vesting), expectations, what happens if someone leaves, how you'll handle disagreement. Silence breeds resentment.
- **Address tension directly and early** — small unspoken frictions compound into ruptures. Regular honest check-ins beat an annual blowup.
- **Clarify who decides what.** Ambiguous authority causes both gridlock and stepped-on toes.

## Leading the team

- **Radical candor:** care personally *and* challenge directly. Caring without challenging is ruinous empathy; challenging without caring is brutal. People need both.
- **Context over control.** Give people the why and the goal; trust them with the how. Micromanagement doesn't scale and corrodes ownership.
- **Communicate relentlessly, especially in hard times.** Founders under-communicate; teams fill silence with worst-case stories. Over-share the vision, the priorities, and the honest state.
- **Model the culture.** Culture is what the founder tolerates and rewards, not what's on the wall. Your behavior under pressure is the real culture.
- **Feedback is a gift, given promptly and specifically.** Both directions — ask for it, and make it safe to give you the truth (founders get filtered information by default).

## Giving hard feedback (a structure)
Be specific (the behavior, not the character), tie it to impact, make it timely (not saved up), and make it a two-way conversation. "When X happened, the effect was Y; help me understand, and here's what I need" lands better than vague or stored-up criticism.
