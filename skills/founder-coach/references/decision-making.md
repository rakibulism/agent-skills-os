# Decision-making under uncertainty

Founders make high-stakes decisions with incomplete information, constantly. The goal is not to be right every time — impossible — but to have a sound *process*, decide at the right speed, and learn fast when wrong.

## Reversible vs. irreversible (the master distinction)

- **Two-way doors (reversible):** if it's wrong, you can undo it at modest cost. Decide *fast*, with ~70% of the information you wish you had. Waiting costs more than the occasional reversal. Most decisions are these; founders fatally over-deliberate them.
- **One-way doors (irreversible):** hard or impossible to undo (selling the company, a co-founder split, a brand-defining choice). Slow down, gather more, get more input, sleep on it.

Misclassifying is the common error: agonizing over reversible calls and rushing irreversible ones. First ask which kind it is.

## A clean structure for a hard call

1. **Define the actual decision.** Strip it to the real choice. Often the framing ("A or B?") hides better options.
2. **Name what matters** — the few criteria that should drive it (impact on PMF, runway, team, values, optionality). Decide the criteria *before* weighing options to avoid rationalizing a favorite.
3. **Generate real options** — push past the binary; the third option is often best. Include "do nothing" and "do the cheap reversible test first."
4. **Weigh trade-offs honestly**, including second-order effects and who's affected.
5. **Check your gut against the analysis.** Strong intuition that conflicts with the analysis is information — dig into why. Experienced pattern-recognition and cold reasoning should both get a vote.
6. **Decide, set a review date, and commit.** State what you expect to see; revisit then.

## Decide, then commit (even amid disagreement)

Indecision has a cost — it stalls the team and forecloses learning. "Disagree and commit" lets you move: make the call, ask dissenters to commit fully, and revisit with data. A reversible decision made now and corrected beats a perfect decision made too late.

## Avoid the traps

- **Analysis paralysis** — waiting for certainty that never comes. Set an information bar and a deadline.
- **Sunk-cost thinking** — choosing based on what you've already spent rather than the future. Past investment is gone; decide forward.
- **Loudest-voice / HiPPO bias** — deciding by who's most insistent rather than what's true. Seek the quiet dissent.
- **Anchoring on the first option or framing.** Reframe before you choose.
- **Deciding while flooded** — fear, exhaustion, and anger distort judgment. For irreversible calls especially, don't decide in the spike; create distance.
- **Confirmation bias** — actively seek the strongest case *against* your leaning before committing.

## When you're wrong
You will be. The skill is detecting it early (that's why you set a review date and success signals) and correcting without ego or delay. A founder who updates fast outperforms one who's right more often but slow to admit error.
