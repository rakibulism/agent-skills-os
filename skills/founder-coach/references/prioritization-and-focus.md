# Prioritization & focus

The founder's scarcest resources are time, attention, and energy — not money. Startups rarely die from doing too little; they die from doing too many things, none of them well. The core leadership act is choosing what *not* to do.

## Find the one thing

At any moment, one thing most determines whether the company lives or wins — the true bottleneck. Usually:
- **Pre-PMF:** finding product-market fit. Almost nothing else matters; partnerships, PR, hiring sprees, and process are distractions until people want the product.
- **Post-PMF:** the constraint on the growth engine (a leaky funnel stage, a hiring gap, an economics problem).

Name that one thing, point the company's best energy at it, and consciously deprioritize the rest. The discipline is saying no to good opportunities to protect the great one.

## Tools for cutting

- **Eisenhower (urgent × important):** Do the important-urgent now; *schedule* the important-not-urgent (this is where strategy lives and founders neglect it); delegate the urgent-not-important; delete the rest. Founders live in "urgent" and starve "important."
- **The "hell yes or no" filter** (Sivers): if a new commitment isn't a clear "hell yes," it's a no. Protects against death by a thousand mild yeses.
- **One Metric That Matters:** focus the company on the single number tied to the current bottleneck; revisit as it moves.
- **Not-doing list:** explicitly list what you're choosing *not* to do this quarter. Visible "no" prevents drift.

## Leverage the founder's time

- **Do what only you can do.** Founder-unique work: vision, key hires, fundraising, the most important customer/product calls, culture. Delegate or drop the rest, even if you'd do it better — your time spent on delegable work is the real cost.
- **Delegate outcomes, not tasks.** Give context and the goal; let people own the how. Hovering doesn't scale and signals distrust.
- **Beware the comfort trap.** Founders flee the scary, important work (a hard conversation, the unproven channel, the strategic bet) into the comfortable, busy work (inbox, tweaks, meetings). Busyness can be avoidance. Ask: am I doing this because it matters most, or because it's easy and I look productive?

## Protect deep focus

- Batch shallow work; defend blocks for the few things that move the needle.
- Reduce decision fatigue by routinizing the trivial (Goodhart's "save your judgment for what matters").
- Fewer, better priorities beat a long list — if everything is a priority, nothing is.

## The weekly reset
A simple recurring practice: What's the one thing that matters most right now? Am I spending my time on it? What can I drop, delegate, or delay? What hard thing am I avoiding? This five-minute reflection prevents weeks lost to the merely urgent.
