# Product-market fit

PMF is the moment the market pulls the product out of your hands. Before it, nothing else matters; after it, almost everything gets easier. The biggest startup mistakes come from scaling before it or from fooling yourself that you have it.

## What PMF actually is

A product that a specific market wants so much that growth and retention happen with relatively little force. Marc Andreessen: "you can always feel when product-market fit isn't happening" — usage is slow, word of mouth is dead, sales cycles drag, churn is high — "and you can always feel when it is" — usage outpaces servers, money piles up, you're hiring as fast as you can.

## Signals you can measure (don't rely on vibes alone)

- **Retention curve flattens.** Plot the % of a cohort still active over time. If it decays to zero, no PMF. If it flattens at a meaningful plateau, a real set of people keep coming back — the strongest signal. Compare cohorts over time.
- **The Sean Ellis test.** Survey active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this?" If **≥40% say "very disappointed,"** that's a common PMF benchmark. Below ~25–30%, keep searching.
- **Organic pull / word of mouth.** Users invite others, growth happens without paid push, you have a waitlist or inbound demand.
- **Usage intensity.** People use it often, deeply, and would be genuinely annoyed to lose it.
- **Unit economics that can work** — even early, signs that you can acquire customers for less than they're worth.

Quantitative + qualitative together: the numbers tell you *whether*, the conversations tell you *why* and *for whom*.

## Find the fit before forcing growth

- **Narrow to who loves it.** Often PMF exists for a *subset* of users. Find the segment that retains and is enthusiastic, and double down on them rather than chasing lukewarm masses.
- **Improve retention before acquisition.** Acquisition without retention is a leaky bucket. The first growth lever is keeping people, not getting them.
- **The "love" bar.** Aim for some users who would be very disappointed to lose it — a few people who love it beats many who like it. Intense desire from a few is more fertile than mild interest from many.

## Pivots

If after honest effort the signals aren't there, change strategy — that's a pivot, not a failure. Types: zoom-in (a feature becomes the product), zoom-out (the product becomes a feature of something bigger), customer-segment, problem, business-model, channel, or technology. A pivot keeps one foot planted (something you've learned is true) while changing the rest. Decide with evidence and a pre-set bar, not despair or stubbornness.

## Don't fool yourself

- A spike from a launch or press is not PMF; check whether they *stay*.
- Friends and early enthusiasts aren't the market; watch behavior of strangers.
- "It's growing" can mask terrible retention if you're acquiring faster than you churn — look at cohorts, not totals.
- PMF is not permanent; markets and competition shift. Keep measuring.
