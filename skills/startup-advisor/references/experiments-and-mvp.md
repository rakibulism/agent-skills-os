# Experiments & the MVP

A startup makes progress by converting assumptions into knowledge as cheaply and quickly as possible. The MVP is just one tool for that; often a lighter test learns the same thing faster.

## Start from the riskiest assumption

List the beliefs the business rests on:
- **Problem** — customers have this problem and care.
- **Solution** — our approach actually solves it.
- **Demand/willingness to pay** — they'll adopt and/or pay.
- **Channel** — we can reach them affordably.
- **Economics** — the numbers work at scale.

Rank by *if this is wrong, how dead are we × how unsure are we*. Test the top one first. There's no point perfecting the solution if no one has the problem.

## Match the test to the assumption (cheapest signal wins)

| To test | Use |
|---|---|
| Do they have the problem? | customer interviews (Mom Test style) |
| Will they want it? | landing page / smoke test / "fake door" (measure clicks, sign-ups) |
| Will they pay? | pre-sale, deposit, paid pilot, letter of intent |
| Does the solution work / is it usable? | concierge MVP (do it manually), Wizard-of-Oz (manual behind a real-looking UI), prototype |
| Does it retain? | a real but minimal product with a cohort of users |
| Which channel works? | small paid tests, one channel at a time |

The order matters: validate demand before building the machine.

## The MVP, correctly understood

The Minimum Viable Product is the smallest thing that lets you start the learning loop with real users — *minimum* (no more than needed to test the bet) and *viable* (enough that the signal is trustworthy). It is not a crappy v1 of the dream; it's an experiment with a hypothesis.

- **Concierge MVP** — manually deliver the service to a few customers. You learn the problem deeply and test demand with near-zero build.
- **Wizard of Oz** — a real-looking product with humans doing the work behind the curtain. Tests the experience without the engineering.
- **Single-feature MVP** — the one feature that delivers the core value, done well, nothing else.
- **Pre-sale / crowdfunding** — sell it before you build it; payment is the ultimate validation.

Cut scope, never the quality of the core path — a broken MVP teaches you it's broken, not whether the idea works.

## Run it like an experiment

For each test, write it down before you start:
- **Hypothesis:** "We believe [X] for [customer]."
- **Test:** what you'll do.
- **Metric & threshold:** the number that means yes (e.g., "≥10% of visitors leave an email," "3 of 10 pre-pay").
- **Decision rule:** persevere / pivot / kill based on the result.

Setting the bar in advance stops you from rationalizing a weak result into a green light.

## Speed is the moat
The startup advantage is learning faster than anyone else. Minimize the total time through Build→Measure→Learn: smaller tests, faster cycles, quicker decisions. A team that runs ten honest experiments while a competitor builds one big bet usually wins.
