# Startup metrics

Measure the few things that tell you the truth at your current stage. The wrong metrics (the ones that only go up) feel good and teach nothing.

## Vanity vs. actionable

- **Vanity:** cumulative signups, total users, downloads, pageviews, registered accounts, raised capital, press mentions. They rise regardless of health.
- **Actionable:** retention, active usage, activation rate, conversion, CAC, LTV, payback, churn, cohort behavior. They can fall — and that's what makes them honest.
- Test: *if this number stalled or dropped, would it tell me to change something?* If not, it's vanity.

## What matters by stage

**Pre-PMF — engagement & retention**
- **Retention curve** (cohort % still active over time) — does it flatten at a meaningful level? This is the single most important early metric.
- **Activation rate** — % of new users who reach first real value ("aha moment").
- **Engagement** — frequency and depth among active users (DAU/MAU ratio for habitual products).
- Qualitative PMF signals (Sean Ellis "very disappointed" %, word of mouth).
Don't obsess over CAC/LTV yet — there's nothing to scale until people stay.

**Post-PMF — growth & economics**
- **CAC** (fully-loaded cost to acquire a paying customer) by channel.
- **LTV** (gross-margin contribution over the customer's life). A common health bar is **LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1**.
- **CAC payback period** — months to recoup CAC from a customer; shorter means less capital to grow (often <12 months for SMB, longer tolerated for enterprise).
- **Churn / net revenue retention.** For recurring revenue, **NRR > 100%** (expansion outweighs churn) means you grow even with zero new customers — a powerful signal.
- **Gross margin** — determines how much of revenue actually funds growth.
- **Growth rate** — and whether it's accelerating or decelerating.

## Frameworks

- **AARRR (pirate metrics):** Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral → Revenue. Map your funnel, find the stage with the biggest leak, fix that one. Optimizing a non-bottleneck stage wastes effort.
- **North Star metric:** one number capturing delivered value (e.g., "weekly active teams," "nights booked"), broken into a few inputs the team can move. Aligns everyone without a dashboard sprawl.
- **One metric that matters (OMTM):** at any moment, focus the company on the single metric tied to the current bottleneck; change it as the bottleneck moves.

## Reading the numbers honestly

- **Cohorts over aggregates.** Totals hide churn; a cohort view shows whether each new group behaves better or worse than the last.
- **Segment.** An average can hide a loved-by-a-niche, ignored-by-everyone-else reality. Find who retains.
- **Leading vs. lagging.** Revenue lags; activation and engagement lead. Instrument leading indicators so you learn in days.
- **Beware Goodhart.** When a metric becomes a target, it gets gamed. Pair growth metrics with quality guardrails (e.g., growth + retention, not growth alone).

## Default dashboard
Most early startups can run on: weekly active users (or the usage that signals value), cohort retention, activation rate, and — once selling — CAC, LTV, payback, and churn. Add only what informs a decision.
