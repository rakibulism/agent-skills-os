# Customer discovery

The first job of a startup is not to build — it's to learn whether a real customer has a real problem worth solving. Almost every founder skips or fakes this and pays for it later.

## Get out of the building

You cannot discover the truth from inside your own head. Talk to the people who have the problem. The goal is to understand their world, their current behavior, and the pain — not to pitch your idea. Aim for patterns across many conversations, not validation from one.

## The Mom Test (how to ask)

Named for a simple idea: ask questions even your mom couldn't lie to you about. Rules:
- **Talk about their life, not your idea.** "Walk me through the last time you dealt with X" beats "would you use a tool that does X?"
- **Ask about the past, not the future.** What people *did* is data; what they *say they'd do* is fiction. "I would definitely buy that" means nothing.
- **Dig into specifics and emotion.** "How do you handle this today? How long does it take? What's the worst part? What have you tried? How much does it cost you?"
- **Avoid leading and pitching.** The moment you describe your solution, they switch to being polite. Stay curious about their problem.
- **Listen for what they've already done about it** — searched for solutions, hacked something together, paid for a workaround. Action is the strongest signal of real pain.

Compliments, hypotheticals, and "that's cool" are not data. Commitments (time, money, reputation, intro to their boss) are.

## Distinguish a real problem from a nice-to-have

- **Frequency** — how often does it occur? Daily pain pulls harder than yearly.
- **Intensity** — how much does it hurt (time, money, risk, emotion)?
- **Existing spend/effort** — are they already paying or working around it? That proves the budget and the will exist.
- **Hair-on-fire test** — would they be relieved to have it solved *now*, or is it a someday-maybe?

A painkiller (urgent, must-solve) beats a vitamin (nice, easy to defer) every time.

## Who exactly?

Define the early customer narrowly — the specific segment with the most acute version of the problem (your beachhead). "Everyone" is not a customer. The narrower and more desperate the early adopter, the easier to find, talk to, and sell.

## How many conversations?

There's no magic number, but you're looking for *patterns* — when the same problems, words, and workarounds keep recurring, you're learning something real. A handful of conversations rarely suffices; dozens reveal the pattern. Stop when new conversations stop surprising you.

## Output of discovery
You should be able to state: who the customer is, the specific painful problem (in their words), how they solve it today and why that's bad, how often and how much it costs them, and evidence they'd act. If you can't, you're not ready to build — you're ready to talk to more people.
