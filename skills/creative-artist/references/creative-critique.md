# Creative critique

Creative work is subjective but not arbitrary — you can judge it on whether it does what it's trying to do, with force and originality. Critique to make the work braver, not safer.

## What to judge

- **Charge / voltage.** Does it create a reaction — surprise, recognition, tension, delight — or does it just sit there? Flat work is the most common failure.
- **Originality, structural not cosmetic.** Is the *idea* fresh, or is a tired idea wearing new paint? Strip the styling and ask if anything's left.
- **Truth / resonance.** Does it feel true, even if fantastical? The best invented things obey an inner logic the audience can feel.
- **Specificity.** Vague work is weak work. Are there concrete, particular, sensory details, or only general gestures?
- **Coherence.** Do the parts serve one effect, or is it a pile of cool moves pulling different directions?
- **Fit to brief.** Does it serve the actual want (the feeling, the audience, the purpose) — or only the maker's cleverness?
- **Restraint.** Is anything overdone? Often the bravest edit is subtraction: cut the explanation, the second twist, the third adjective.

## How to give the note

- **React first, diagnose second.** Say what the work made you feel or fail to feel, then trace it to a cause. "It lost me here, because the metaphor switched" is usable; "I don't like it" is not.
- **Protect the risk.** When work is bold but flawed, fix the flaw without sanding off the boldness. The instinct to make things "safe" kills more good work than any other note.
- **Push toward braver, not blander.** If the choice is between two options, favor the one with more conviction. A strong wrong is more fixable than a weak right.
- **Be specific about the fix-direction, open about the execution.** Name the problem and a direction; leave room for the maker to solve it their way.

## Self-critique checklist before calling it done

- Would this stop me if I scrolled past it cold?
- Is the core idea new, or just the surface?
- Can the audience picture it from the details, or am I relying on adjectives?
- Did I cut everything that isn't pulling weight?
- Does it serve the feeling the brief was reaching for?
- Did I stop at the first good idea, or push to a better one?

## The revision mindset

First drafts exist to be killed and mined. Generate beyond what you need, choose the strongest, and develop it past where you'd normally stop. The difference between competent and remarkable is almost always one more honest revision.
