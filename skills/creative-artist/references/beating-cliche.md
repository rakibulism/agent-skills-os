# Beating cliché

Cliché is the path of least resistance — the idea your brain offers first because it's offered everyone first. It isn't wrong; it's just used up. The cure is to name it, then route around it.

## Why first ideas are clichés

The most available idea is the most common one, because availability is shared. "Lightbulb = idea," "handshake = partnership," "mountain = challenge," "broken chains = freedom." If it came instantly, assume it came to everyone.

## The naming step

Before generating real options, write the 3–5 ideas anyone would have for this brief. This does two things: it clears them out of your head, and it gives you a list to deliberately avoid or subvert. You can't dodge a cliché you haven't named.

## Tactics to escape

- **Subvert it.** Take the cliché and invert, exaggerate, or literalize it. If everyone shows the summit, show the descent. If "fast" is always a cheetah, make it a sloth that's somehow already there.
- **Go oblique.** Approach the idea from the side. To convey grief, don't show tears — show the second toothbrush no one threw away.
- **Get hyper-specific.** Clichés are general ("a happy family"). Truth is specific ("a dad pretending the burnt pancake is the good kind"). Specificity is inherently un-clichéd because particulars are unique.
- **Change the frame.** Shift medium, scale, point of view, or time. The familiar becomes strange when you change where you stand.
- **Find the second-order idea.** The cliché is the first association. Ask what the cliché is *really* about, then find a fresher carrier for that same meaning.
- **Embrace the constraint that bans it.** "Convey luck without a four-leaf clover, horseshoe, or dice." Removing the obvious tokens forces real invention.

## Cliché in language (for writing/copy)

Kill: "in today's fast-paced world," "think outside the box," "game-changer," "at the end of the day," "journey," "unlock," "elevate," "seamless," "revolutionary," "passionate about." These are placeholders where a real thought should be. Replace each with something concrete and earned.

## The test

A non-cliché idea should make the audience feel one of: *I haven't seen that before*, *that's true and I never noticed*, or *that shouldn't work but it does*. If the reaction is *yeah, makes sense* with no spark — keep going. You found the obvious, not the original.
