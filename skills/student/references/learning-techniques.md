# Learning techniques that work

Ranked roughly by evidence and impact. The top of this list is where to spend your effort; the bottom is what most students do instead.

## High impact (do these)

**Retrieval practice (active recall)**
Trying to recall information *strengthens* the memory far more than re-encountering it. Close the book and write/say everything you can about a topic; then check and fill gaps. Flashcards, blank-page brain-dumps, practice questions, and "explain it from memory" all count. The struggle to retrieve is the mechanism — don't peek too soon.

**Spaced repetition**
Reviewing at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, a week, two weeks…) just as you're about to forget produces dramatically better long-term retention than the same total time massed together. Tools like Anki automate the scheduling; a paper system or calendar works too. The key is *distributing* practice across days, not the specific algorithm.

**Interleaving**
Instead of practicing one type of problem in a block (AAAA BBBB), mix them (ABCABC). It feels harder and slower — and it produces better learning and, crucially, the ability to *recognize which method a problem needs*. Block practice teaches execution; interleaving teaches selection, which is what real tests and real life demand.

## Medium impact (good supports)

**Elaboration**
Ask and answer "why?" and "how does this connect to what I already know?" Linking new material to existing knowledge and to other new material builds a network that's easier to recall and apply. Generate your own examples.

**Self-explanation**
While studying, explain to yourself what each step means and why it follows. Verbalizing the reasoning surfaces gaps and deepens encoding.

**Dual coding**
Combine verbal and visual representations — diagrams, timelines, concept maps, sketches alongside text. Two complementary encodings give two retrieval routes.

**Concrete examples**
Abstract ideas stick when anchored to specific, vivid examples — and multiple varied examples teach the underlying principle better than one.

## Low impact (what feels productive but isn't, alone)

- **Rereading** — builds fluency/familiarity that masquerades as mastery. You recognize the text but can't reproduce it.
- **Highlighting/underlining** — passive; marking text is not learning text. Worse, it can fragment attention onto isolated phrases.
- **Summarizing** — only helps if done from memory and done well; copying is near-useless.
- **Massed practice ("cramming")** — short-term gain, fast forgetting.

These aren't forbidden — rereading once to get the lay of the land is fine — but they must not be the *main* method. Convert them into active versions: reread once, then close and recall; highlight, then turn highlights into questions.

## The meta-principle: desirable difficulty
Conditions that make studying feel harder in the moment — retrieving instead of reviewing, spacing instead of massing, interleaving instead of blocking — make learning *more durable*. Trust the discomfort; the easy-feeling methods are the ones quietly failing you.
