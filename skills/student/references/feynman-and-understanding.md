# The Feynman technique & real understanding

The fastest way to find out whether you understand something is to try to explain it simply. Confusion hides comfortably in jargon and in reading; it cannot survive a clear explanation to a beginner.

## The Feynman technique, in four steps

1. **Pick the concept** and write its name at the top of a blank page.
2. **Explain it in plain language**, as if teaching a curious 12-year-old. No jargon unless you immediately define it. Use simple words, concrete examples, and analogies.
3. **Find the gaps.** Wherever you stall, hand-wave, or fall back on a memorized phrase you can't unpack — that's exactly what you don't actually understand. Mark it.
4. **Go back to the source** for those gaps only, then re-explain. Repeat until the explanation is smooth and simple.

The output is a clear explanation; the *real* product is the precise map of your own gaps, which tells you exactly what to restudy.

## Understanding vs. memorizing

- **Memorizing** = you can state it. **Understanding** = you can explain *why*, derive it, apply it to a new case, and predict what changes if a condition changes.
- Test for understanding: Can you explain it without notes? Teach it? Give your own example (not the book's)? Predict what happens if you tweak a variable? Connect it to something else? Spot when it *doesn't* apply?
- Memorization has its place (vocabulary, formulas, facts with no derivation) — use spaced repetition for that. But don't memorize what you could understand; understood material is recalled more reliably and transfers to new problems.

## A structure for learning a concept deeply

```
1. Simple version: explain it plainly, with an analogy.
2. Concrete example: a specific, vivid instance.
3. Why it's true / how it works: the mechanism or derivation.
4. Where it breaks: the limits, exceptions, edge cases, common misconceptions.
5. Precise version: the rigorous statement, now that the intuition is in place.
6. Connections: how it links to neighboring ideas.
```

Going intuition-first then precise-after is far stickier than starting from the formal definition.

## Building intuition

- **Analogies** map the new onto the familiar — but always note where the analogy breaks, or it becomes a misconception.
- **Extreme cases** — what happens at zero, at infinity, at the boundary? Limits reveal how something behaves.
- **Vary one thing** — change a single condition and predict the effect; checking the prediction builds a working model.
- **Multiple representations** — see it as words, a picture, an equation, a story. Each angle catches what the others miss.

## The teaching test
If you can teach it — clearly, simply, fielding "but why?" questions — you understand it. If you can only recite it, you've memorized it. Aim for the former, and use explaining (to a friend, a rubber duck, or a blank page) as both a study method and a check.
