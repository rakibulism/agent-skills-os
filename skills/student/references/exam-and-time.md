# Exam prep & time management

Preparation is a logistics problem as much as a learning problem. A good plan turns a vague mountain of material into a sequence of doable, well-aimed sessions.

## Plan backward from the deadline

1. **Inventory** everything to cover and estimate effort per topic.
2. **Weight by yield** — exam likelihood × your current weakness. High-likelihood + weak = top priority; low-likelihood + already-strong = minimal.
3. **Schedule with spacing built in.** Distribute each topic across multiple days with review passes, rather than one big block. Earlier-but-lighter beats later-but-heavier.
4. **Front-load testing.** Schedule practice questions and past papers early, not just at the end — they reveal gaps while there's still time to fix them.
5. **Leave buffer.** Things take longer than planned and life intrudes; an over-packed schedule with no slack collapses on the first bad day.

## Make practice match performance

The strongest predictor of exam performance is practicing under exam-like conditions:
- **Past papers / sample questions**, ideally timed and closed-book.
- Match the *format*: essays need practiced essays; MCQs need practiced MCQs; problem sets need solved problems.
- After each practice test, do an **error review**: for every miss, was it a knowledge gap, a misread, a method error, or time pressure? Each diagnosis has a different fix.

## Focus and energy

- **Focused blocks with breaks.** Work in sustained blocks (e.g., 25–50 min) with short breaks; the exact numbers matter less than protecting the block from distraction. Put the phone in another room.
- **Single-task.** Multitasking shreds retention. One subject, full attention.
- **Match hard material to your peak hours** and routine review to your low-energy times.
- **Sleep is study.** Memory consolidates during sleep; an all-nighter trades the consolidation you need for cramming that won't last. Protect sleep, especially the night before.
- **Exercise, food, and breaks** aren't indulgences — they sustain the cognitive performance the plan depends on.

## Motivation and starting

- **Lower the activation energy.** "Study biology" is daunting; "do 10 recall questions on the heart" is startable. Shrink the next action until it's trivial to begin.
- **Use the 2-minute start.** Commit to just two minutes; momentum usually carries you past it.
- **Make progress visible.** Tick topics off, track questions done; visible progress fuels persistence.
- **Implementation intentions.** "After dinner, at my desk, I will do X" beats "I'll study more." Tie the action to a specific time and place.

## In the exam

- Read instructions and scan the whole paper first; budget time per question by marks available.
- Do the questions you know first to bank marks and build confidence.
- For each question, answer what's *asked* (note the command word — "compare," "evaluate," "calculate").
- Leave time to check; for problems, sanity-check the answer's plausibility.
- If stuck, move on and return — fresh eyes later often see it.

## After the exam
Whatever the result, do a brief review of what worked and what you'd change. The point isn't this exam; it's getting better at learning for the next one.
