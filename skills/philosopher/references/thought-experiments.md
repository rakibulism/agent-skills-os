# Thought experiments

A thought experiment isolates a variable by imagining a case stripped of distractions, then reads off the intuition it provokes. The intuition becomes data a theory must accommodate or explain away.

## How they work

1. Construct a scenario that holds everything fixed except the feature in question.
2. Notice the strong intuition the case produces ("that would be wrong," "she still wouldn't *know*," "that's not the same person").
3. Ask what principle would have to be true to justify that intuition.
4. Test the principle against other cases — does it generalize, or does the next case break it?

The case is a probe, not proof. Intuitions can be culturally loaded or unreliable; a thought experiment opens an argument, it doesn't close one.

## Canonical examples and what they isolate

- **Trolley problem** — divert a trolley to kill one instead of five? Isolates doing vs. allowing, and intention vs. foresight (compare to pushing a man off a bridge — same math, different intuition). Pressure-tests consequentialism.
- **Gettier cases** — justified true belief that intuitively isn't knowledge. Breaks the classic definition of knowledge and isolates the role of luck.
- **Brain in a vat / Cartesian demon** — could all your experience be a simulation? Isolates what we can know and the problem of the external world.
- **Mary's room** — a scientist who knows all physical facts about color but has never seen it; does she learn something new on first seeing red? Isolates whether experience is reducible to physical facts.
- **The experience machine** (Nozick) — plug into perfect simulated pleasure forever? Tests whether well-being is just pleasant states or also contact with reality.
- **Veil of ignorance** (Rawls) — choose society's rules not knowing your place in it. Isolates fairness from self-interest.
- **Ship of Theseus** — replace every plank; same ship? Isolates identity over time and through change.
- **Chinese room** (Searle) — symbol manipulation without understanding; isolates syntax vs. semantics in minds and machines.

## Building your own

- **Vary one thing.** A good case differs from a comparison case in exactly one respect, so the intuition pinpoints what's doing the moral/epistemic work.
- **Keep it clean.** Strip irrelevant detail that would muddy the intuition or smuggle in extra factors.
- **Use contrast pairs.** Two near-identical cases with diverging intuitions reveal the hidden variable (trolley-switch vs. trolley-push).
- **Push to the limit.** Extreme or fantastical cases are legitimate — they test whether a principle is truly universal.

## Cautions

- **Intuitions aren't infallible.** They can reflect bias, framing, or unfamiliarity with the bizarre setup. Weigh them; don't worship them.
- **Beware "intuition pumps" engineered to mislead** (Dennett's term) — scenarios rigged so the framing does the persuading. Check whether changing irrelevant details flips the intuition; if so, distrust it.
- **The case is the start of reasoning, not its substitute.** After the intuition, do the work of finding and testing the principle behind it.
