# Argument analysis

An argument is a set of premises offered in support of a conclusion. Analyzing one means making its structure explicit and then asking two separate questions: does it hold together, and are its parts true.

## Standard form

Rewrite any argument as numbered premises and a conclusion:

```
P1. All humans are mortal.
P2. Socrates is human.
∴  C. Socrates is mortal.
```

The discipline of doing this exposes the **suppressed premise** — the unstated assumption the argument needs. "Taxes are theft because they take your money without consent" hides "taking money without consent is theft," which is the real claim to examine.

## Validity vs. soundness (keep them separate)

- **Valid** — *if* the premises were true, the conclusion would have to be true. This is about structure/form only. ("All cats are reptiles; Felix is a cat; so Felix is a reptile" is valid and false.)
- **Sound** — valid *and* all premises are actually true. Only sound arguments establish their conclusions.
- Strategy: check validity first (is the inference legit?), then attack the weakest true-or-false premise.

For non-deductive arguments, the parallel is **strength** (do the premises make the conclusion probable?) and **cogency** (strong + true premises). Inductive and abductive (inference to the best explanation) arguments are evaluated this way.

## Locate the load-bearing premise

Most arguments stand or fall on one contestable premise. Find it and the debate becomes tractable. Often it's the suppressed one.

## Common fallacies

**Formal (broken structure)**
- *Affirming the consequent:* If P then Q; Q; therefore P. (Invalid.)
- *Denying the antecedent:* If P then Q; not P; therefore not Q. (Invalid.)

**Informal (content/relevance)**
- *Equivocation* — a key term shifts meaning mid-argument.
- *Begging the question* — the conclusion is assumed in a premise.
- *False dilemma* — only two options presented when more exist.
- *Straw man* — refuting a distorted, weaker version of the view.
- *Ad hominem* — attacking the arguer instead of the argument.
- *Appeal to authority/popularity/nature/antiquity* — true because an expert/many/natural/old said so.
- *Slippery slope* — asserting a chain of consequences without justifying each link.
- *Composition/division* — assuming the parts share the whole's properties or vice versa.
- *Post hoc* — after therefore because of.
- *Hasty generalization* — a sweeping claim from too few cases.

Naming a fallacy is the start, not the end: explain *why* the move fails here, since some "fallacies" (e.g., appeal to authority) are reasonable in context.

## Evaluating, charitably

Before objecting: restate the argument so its author would agree. Then ask — is it valid/strong? which premise is weakest? is there a counterexample to a universal claim? does a term equivocate? Offer the strongest objection *and* the best reply the author could give. The goal is to find what's true, not to win.
