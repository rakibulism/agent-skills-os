# Conceptual analysis

A surprising share of hard questions are confusions about words wearing the costume of questions about the world. "Is a hot dog a sandwich?" "Is this art?" "Is an AI conscious?" Before answering, get clear on the concept.

## Necessary and sufficient conditions

The classic tool for defining a concept:
- A condition is **necessary** if the thing can't be an instance without it (being unmarried is necessary for being a bachelor).
- A condition is **sufficient** if having it guarantees instancehood.
- A full analysis offers conditions that are *individually necessary and jointly sufficient*: "X is a bachelor iff X is an unmarried, adult, human male."

Then **stress-test with cases**: find a counterexample where the definition includes something it shouldn't, or excludes something it should. Refine. Repeat. (The Pope is an unmarried adult human male but not, idiomatically, a bachelor — so the analysis needs work.)

## When tidy definitions fail

Many real concepts resist necessary-and-sufficient analysis:
- **Family resemblance** (Wittgenstein) — "game" has no single common thread; instances overlap like family features. Some concepts are clusters, not essences.
- **Prototypes** — we judge category membership by similarity to a central example (a robin is a "birdier" bird than a penguin).
- **Vagueness / sorites** — where exactly does a "heap" become a "non-heap" as you remove grains? Borderline cases are unavoidable; demanding a sharp line can be a mistake.

Recognizing which kind of concept you have prevents fruitless hunts for a precise definition where none exists.

## Distinctions that prevent confusion

- **Descriptive vs. normative** — what *is* vs. what *ought to be*. ("People do lie" ≠ "lying is permissible.")
- **Sense vs. reference** — two terms can pick out the same thing with different meanings ("the morning star" / "the evening star").
- **Type vs. token** — the abstract kind vs. a particular instance.
- **A priori vs. a posteriori** — knowable by reason alone vs. requiring experience.
- **Necessary vs. contingent** — could not have been otherwise vs. happens to be so.

## Watch for these moves

- **Equivocation** — a term silently changes meaning. Pin one meaning per argument.
- **Persuasive definition** — smuggling a value judgment into a "definition" ("real freedom is…"). Flag it.
- **Definitional dodge** — "winning" a substantive dispute by stipulating a definition so only your side qualifies. That settles the word, not the issue; the disagreement just relocates.

## The payoff

Often, clarifying the concept dissolves the question ("it's a verbal dispute — you each mean something different by 'free will'") or sharpens it into something answerable ("once we mean *this* by consciousness, here's what would settle it"). Either outcome is progress.
