# Ethical frameworks

For a hard moral question, don't reach for one framework as a tribe — run the question through several lenses. They illuminate different features, and where they converge you have a robust answer; where they diverge you've found the real crux.

## The major lenses

**Consequentialism (outcomes)**
Right action = the one producing the best overall results. Utilitarianism: maximize well-being, counting everyone equally.
- *Asks:* what are the actual consequences, for everyone affected, including indirect and long-term?
- *Strengths:* impartial, practical, takes suffering seriously, scales.
- *Strains:* can justify sacrificing the few for the many; demands impartiality that crowds out personal ties; requires predicting outcomes we often can't; "well-being" is hard to measure and compare.

**Deontology (duties, rights, rules)**
Some acts are right or wrong in themselves, regardless of outcome. Kant: act only on maxims you could will as universal law; treat persons always as ends, never merely as means.
- *Asks:* what are my duties? whose rights are at stake? could everyone act this way? am I using someone merely as a tool?
- *Strengths:* protects individuals against being sacrificed; matches strong intuitions about rights, honesty, and dignity.
- *Strains:* rigid in conflicts (two duties collide); can seem to ignore catastrophic consequences; struggles to say where duties come from.

**Virtue ethics (character)**
Focus on being a good person, not just doing right acts. The right action is what a person of practical wisdom (phronesis) would do; cultivate virtues (courage, honesty, justice, temperance) as means between extremes.
- *Asks:* what would a person of good character do? what does this choice make me into? what's the wise mean here?
- *Strengths:* captures the role of habit, emotion, judgment, and context; aims at a whole flourishing life, not isolated acts.
- *Strains:* less action-guiding in a tight spot; "what would the virtuous person do" can be circular; virtues can conflict.

**Contractualism (justifiability to others)**
Right principles are those no one could reasonably reject (Scanlon), or that free, equal people would agree to (Rawls, via the veil of ignorance).
- *Asks:* could I justify this to everyone it affects? would we agree to this rule not knowing our position?
- *Strengths:* grounds fairness and legitimacy; good for institutions and policy.
- *Strains:* who counts as a contractor (animals? future people?); "reasonable" does heavy lifting.

## Using them together

1. State the dilemma and who is affected.
2. Run each lens: best outcome? duties/rights violated? what would the virtuous do? justifiable to all?
3. Note convergence (robust signal) and divergence (the genuine crux — usually a clash between aggregate good and individual rights).
4. Make the trade-off explicit rather than hiding behind one framework. Say which consideration you weight most here and why.

## Applied-ethics checklist (tech, business, policy)

- Who bears the costs and who gets the benefits — and are they the same people?
- Consent: are affected people informed and able to opt out?
- Power asymmetry: who can't say no?
- Reversibility: can harm be undone if we're wrong?
- Distribution: who is made worse off, especially the already-vulnerable?
- Precedent: what norm does this set if it becomes standard?
- Second-order effects and incentives created.

## Stance

After a fair, multi-lens hearing, it's legitimate to conclude one answer is better-supported — moral reasoning aims at getting it right, not at endless even-handedness. State your considered view, the strongest objection to it, and what would change your mind.
